Background

The Ottoman Empire under Sultan Mahmut II (1808–1839) was a time of foundational change as the Sultan sought to modernize his empire. The Auspicious Incident of 1826 — in which the Sultan disbanded the Janissary Corps — kicked off a sweeping series of reforms that would become the Tanzimat ("Reorganization"). With the old military order dissolved, the Sultan required a new, formalized way to monitor the population for the purposes of drafting soldiers and taxing the draft-exempt population: the ahl al-kitab (dhimmi), the People of the Book — Jews and Christians.

This census was intended to act as a living register, periodically updated as males were born, moved away, or died, serving both military and tax purposes. These censuses recorded exclusively males; daughters and wives were not recorded until much later in the Hamidian period (1876–1909).

About This Census

The first real census of the Ottoman Empire was completed in 1247 AH (11 June 1831 – 20 May 1832 CE). It mainly covered the heartland of the Empire. The format was not standardized beyond recording tax status, given name, father's name (for the head of household or non-sons), and age. Some census takers recorded occupations and additional comments. Additions — as well as notations of whether an individual had died or moved away — can also be found in different hands, along with the date of registration.

A Note on Surnames: In the Ottoman Empire, there were officially no surnames, even though much of the Jewish community and many Christian communities had stable family names. The absence of surnames on most 19th-century documents reflects this general rule — though it was not always followed. This census is notable for the partial survival of a third register in which the Ottoman official did record surnames.

Discovery & Structure of the Records

While conducting archival research in Istanbul in 2022, Michael Waas (Associate Director of the Sephardic Research Division) discovered that for Izmir, three separate copies of this first census register survive. Two of them are complete and appear to be copies of one another to some degree. Together they record over 3,000 men and boys. Neither of these contains surnames.

The third register is the most remarkable: the Ottoman official who compiled it recorded surnames. This register is fragmented — approximately one-third of the original survives — but from it, more than 230 unique surnames have been extracted. All names are recorded in Ottoman Turkish script.

Fields Recorded

The standard fields captured across the census registers include:
