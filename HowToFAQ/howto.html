Step-by-Step Guide

How to JewishGen & FAQ

Everything you need to know to get started with JewishGen

Start at Home

Before you type a single name into the search bar, pause. The most important step in genealogy actually happens offline. Genealogy is all about working from the known to the unknown.

  • Write it down: Jot down everything you already know about yourself, your parents, and your grandparents.
  • Gather the basics: Get the names, dates (birth, marriage, death), and locations organized first. This is your foundation.

Find the Paper Trail

To research your family pre-immigration, you first need to find them in the records created after they emigrated.

  • Be a detective: Dig into old family papers, naturalization records, ship manifests, vital records, and even the backs of old photos.
  • The ultimate goal: You are looking for two golden nuggets of information — your ancestor's pre-immigration name and the specific town they left behind.

Throw Perfect Spelling Out the Window

When searching for your ancestors, flexibility is your best friend.

  • Names evolve: It is a stubborn myth that names were changed by clerks at Ellis Island. In reality, names simply shifted over time, across borders, and depending on who was holding the pen. Read more about the Ellis Island naming myth.
  • Sound it out: A single surname could be spelled half a dozen ways. First names often morphed from Yiddish or Hebrew into local forms even before emigration. Prepare to get creative and embrace phonetic variations!

Forget Countries, Focus on Towns

Historical borders shifted constantly. Depending on the year a document was signed, your ancestor might have listed their home as Russia, Poland, or Austria — without ever moving!

  • Towns are the key: Pinning down the specific town name is vital. Even if it is horribly misspelled on a ship manifest, finding that town unlocks one of JewishGen's most powerful tools — more on that later!

Start with the Unified Search

Think of the JewishGen Unified Search as your wide-angle lens. Instead of guessing which specific historical collection your ancestor might be hiding in, this tool searches almost all of JewishGen's databases at once. It is the absolute best starting line for any beginner.

The "Less is More" Rule

It is incredibly tempting to fill out every single box on the search form — first name, last name, town, and dates. Resist the urge! Historical records are notoriously incomplete, and if you restrict your search too heavily, you may accidentally filter out the very record you need.

  • Start broad: Search with just a surname first.
  • Narrow gradually: If you get an overwhelming number of results, add a first name. If you still have too many, then try adding a geographic region or town.

Trust the Phonetic Search

By default, our search engine uses a setting called "Phonetically (Beider-Morse)." Leave it exactly as it is! This system is specifically designed for Eastern European and Jewish names. It mathematically groups names that sound similar or share linguistic roots, ensuring you don't miss a vital record just because a clerk swapped a "v" for a "w" or a "tz" for a "z".

Searching for an "Exact" spelling is the most common mistake beginners make.

How to Read Your Results

When you hit search, JewishGen intelligently organizes your results by category and historical region — for example, "Poland Database," "Holocaust Database," or "Burial Registry."

  • Look for the numbers: The count next to each category tells you exactly how many matches were found in that specific collection.
  • Click to expand: Select a category to view the actual transcribed records.

Dive Into the Details

Once you expand a category, the title of the collection itself is your key to learning more. Clicking on that title will open up all the crucial context you need — where the information came from, the history of the dataset, and instructions on how to obtain copies of the original documents for your own research files.

Remember: most of our entries are simply indexes. There may be additional information in the original record itself!

Search Operators: Secret Shortcuts

JewishGen's search bar understands several special operators that give you precise control over your results. Once you know them, you'll use them constantly.

  • Comma = OR: Search for either name. Example: Schwartz, Weiss (finds either)
  • Space = AND: Search for both names. Example: Schwartz Weiss (finds both)
  • Wildcards (*): Fill in blanks for bad handwriting. Example: Sp*l*ng (finds Spelling, Sperling)
  • Brackets [ ]: Force a specific starting letter. Example: [F]isch (finds Fish, not Visz)
  • Synonyms: Exact search includes standard variants. Example: Jankel = Jakob = Jacob = Jack
  • Starts With / Contains: Finds root words. Example: "Gold" finds FeinGold

The Communities Database

Finding the name of your ancestral town on a passenger manifest is a huge victory, but it is only the beginning. The JewishGen Communities Database is your ultimate historical atlas. Search for the town, and you will get a complete geographic and historical profile — all the different phonetic spellings of the town's name, which empires controlled it over the centuries, and exactly where it sits on a modern map. It transforms a scribbled word on an old document into a real, physical place.

The Index is Just a Signpost

When you find a match in our databases, you are usually looking at an index — a translated summary of the most important facts — rather than a full transcription. While our indexes are incredibly detailed, the original document almost always holds more secrets.

An original birth or marriage register might list the names of witnesses (who are often relatives!), the street address of the family home, the father's exact occupation, or even a scribbled note in the margin about a later emigration. Always use the JewishGen index as your map to hunt down a copy of the original document!

KehilaLinks: Virtual Hometowns

Once you locate your town in the Communities Database, look for a link to its KehilaLink. These are custom, volunteer-created web pages dedicated to specific Jewish communities — think of them as virtual heritage museums. They are frequently packed with historical photos, street maps, local histories, and personal family stories from other descendants that you simply will not find in official government archives.

Yizkor Books: Reading Their Stories

Government vital records tell you when your ancestors lived and died, but they don't tell you how they lived. To understand the vibrant fabric of their daily lives — the bustling markets, the rival synagogues, the local characters, and the heartbreaking tragedies — you need to read a Yizkor Book.

Written by survivors and former residents after the Holocaust, these memorial books capture the essence of destroyed Jewish communities. JewishGen offers hundreds of full English translations to read for free, providing unparalleled emotional context for your family tree.

Research Divisions: Localized Expertise

The world is vast, and record-keeping habits varied wildly from country to country and era to era. JewishGen is organized into smaller, highly focused regional teams called Research Divisions (formerly referred to as Special Interest Groups, or SIGs). Whether your family was from Poland, Ukraine, Germany, or Hungary, there is a dedicated group of experts and volunteers focusing specifically on the records from that area. Exploring your region's Research Division is the best way to break through a geographic brick wall.

Foreign Language Records & AI Tools

Tracing Jewish roots means encountering a beautifully complex mix of languages and scripts — Russian Cyrillic, Hebrew, Yiddish, Polish, Romanian, and more. Don't be intimidated.

A word of caution about AI translation tools: they can be useful, but only if you are knowledgeable about their limitations. If you choose to use AI, be exacting with your prompts and always verify results against other sources. For accurate, nuanced translation by people who understand the genealogical context, use ViewMate — JewishGen's free community translation service.

Reading a Jewish Grave

Cemetery records are among the most overlooked genealogical sources — and among the most revealing. A Jewish headstone is far more than a name and two dates. Hebrew inscriptions often include the deceased's Hebrew name, their father's name, their community of origin, and sometimes moving tributes that survive nowhere else.

Learning to read Hebrew tombstone inscriptions — or finding someone who can — opens a door to information that never made it into any government archive. Read our guide to reading Jewish tombstone inscriptions.

The Burial Registry (JOWBR)

The JewishGen Online Worldwide Burial Registry contains over 7 million burial records from more than 12,000 cemeteries across 144 countries. Compiled entirely by volunteers, JOWBR links burial records to headstone photographs and, where available, Hebrew inscription transcriptions.

Search JOWBR not just to confirm that an ancestor is buried somewhere — search it to find details, Hebrew names, and family connections that exist nowhere else.

The JewishGen Family Finder (JGFF)

Before you spend hundreds of hours researching a specific family line, check if someone else has already done the work! The JGFF is not a database of historical records — it is a database of researchers.

  • Search first: Enter your family surnames and ancestral towns. If another user is researching that exact combination, you can contact them directly. You might just find a second cousin who already has the old family photos you've been looking for!
  • Plant your flag: It is crucial that you register your own surnames and towns in the JGFF. If you don't list what you are looking for, your long-lost relatives won't be able to find you when they start their own search.

Discussion Groups: Tap Into the Global Brain

When you are staring at a confusing document and feeling completely stuck, it is time to turn to the JewishGen Discussion Group — our global forum where thousands of experts, volunteers, and enthusiastic beginners have been gathering for decades to help each other out. You can also connect with the wider community through the Jewish Genealogy Portal on Facebook.

  • Crowdsource your brick walls: Can't figure out a shifting historical border? Need help interpreting a strange note on a ship manifest? Post your question. Genealogists absolutely love solving mysteries — especially other people's mysteries!

ViewMate: Your Translation Lifeline

Tracing Jewish roots means encountering a beautifully complex mix of languages — from Russian and Polish to Yiddish and Hebrew. When you find a document you cannot read, do not panic — and do not rely blindly on AI!

Post an image of the record to our ViewMate tool. Our incredible community of multilingual volunteers will help you translate the nuances accurately, completely free of charge.

JewishGen Education: Level Up Your Skills

Genealogy is a craft that requires constant learning. If you want to move beyond the basics, JewishGen offers interactive, online classes taught by expert genealogists. Whether you want to learn how to decipher Russian Cyrillic, understand Holocaust research, or organize your growing family tree, our educational courses provide structured, step-by-step guidance.

JewishGen Talks: Learn from the Experts

If you are looking for ongoing inspiration and specialized knowledge, tune into JewishGen Talks. This continuous series of virtual lectures and webinars brings top genealogists, historians, and archivists directly to your screen. It is the perfect way to stay updated on new database additions, learn advanced search strategies, and explore the broader historical context of your ancestors' lives.

Conferences: Take the Search Offline

Some of the biggest genealogical breakthroughs happen in the hallways between lectures. JewishGen is deeply involved in major international events like the IAJGS International Conference on Jewish Genealogy and RootsTech. Attending these conferences is an unparalleled opportunity to meet fellow researchers face-to-face, attend expert-led sessions, and talk directly with JewishGen leadership.

The Kalikow Center: Your NYC Research Headquarters

Sometimes, there is no substitute for in-person research. If you are in New York City, or planning a visit, the Peter and Mary Kalikow Jewish Genealogy Research Center is your physical gateway to the archives. Visitors can access exclusive offline databases, utilize specialized library collections, and receive hands-on guidance from our expert staff genealogists.

Pay It Forward: Become a Volunteer

Every translated index, KehilaLink, and transcribed cemetery record on JewishGen exists because a volunteer decided to give back. Once you have used the site to find your own ancestors, consider paying it forward! You don't need to be a professional genealogist or fluent in five languages to help. We always need volunteers to transcribe indexes, photograph local cemeteries, and share their expertise.
