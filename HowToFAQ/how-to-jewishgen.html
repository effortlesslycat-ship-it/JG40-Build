How to JewishGen

A step-by-step guide to researching your Jewish family history — and answers to the questions every newcomer asks.

Start at Home

Before you type a single name into the search bar, pause! The most important step in genealogy actually happens offline. Genealogy is all about working from the known to the unknown.

  • Write it down: Jot down everything you already know about yourself, your parents, and your grandparents.
  • Gather the basics: Get the names, dates (birth, marriage, death), and locations organized first. This is your foundation.

Find the Paper Trail

To jump the pond and research your family in Europe, you first need to find them in the records created after they emigrated.

  • Be a detective: Dig into old family papers, naturalization records, ship manifests, vital records, and even the backs of old photos.
  • The ultimate goal: You are looking for two golden nuggets of information — your ancestor's pre-immigration name and the specific town they left behind.

Throw Perfect Spelling Out the Window

When searching for your ancestors, flexibility is your best friend.

  • Names evolve: It's a stubborn myth that names were changed by clerks at Ellis Island. In reality, names simply shifted over time, across borders, and depending on who was holding the pen.
  • Sound it out: A single surname could be spelled half a dozen ways. First names often morphed from Yiddish or Hebrew into local forms even before emigration. Prepare to get creative and embrace phonetic variations!

Forget Countries, Focus on Towns

Historical borders shifted constantly. Depending on the year a document was signed, your ancestor might have listed their home as Russia, Poland, or Austria — without ever moving!

  • Towns are the key: Pinning down the specific town name is vital. Even if it's horribly misspelled on a ship manifest, finding that town is the master key to unlocking JewishGen's most powerful databases.

Frequently Asked Questions

These are some of the questions we hear most often — from researchers just starting out and from experienced genealogists who've hit a wall.

The most common culprit isn't a missing record — it's a spelling mismatch. Your family's name may appear in dozens of different forms across different documents, depending on the country, the language, who was writing, and when.

There's also a persistent myth worth putting to rest: names were not changed by clerks at Ellis Island. Immigration inspectors worked from passenger manifests prepared before departure and generally recorded names phonetically as they heard them. The real transformation happened gradually — as families moved between countries, translated names into new languages, or simply chose new American-sounding names for themselves.

The key is to search phonetically and broadly. JewishGen's databases use Soundex and Daitch-Mokotoff Soundex — systems designed specifically to capture the many phonetic variations of Jewish names. When in doubt, try searching with just the first few letters of a surname, or experiment with alternate vowel combinations.

This is one of the most disorienting aspects of Jewish research — and it trips up even experienced genealogists. The same town might appear as Russian, Polish, Austrian, or German in different records, even though the town itself never moved. Borders were redrawn constantly, especially after World War I and World War II.

JewishGen addresses this by organizing communities around the town itself, not the changing political borders. The Communities Database includes alternate names in multiple languages, cross-referenced across historical periods — so you can find the same place whether it was recorded as Lwów, Lviv, Lemberg, or Leopolis.

The practical rule: always search by town name, not country. Use the Town Finder to locate your ancestral community and see its full naming history before diving into records.

Don't start searching JewishGen's databases just yet — first, build the bridge. The key is to find your ancestor in records created after immigration, since many of those documents ask for a place of origin.

Naturalization records are often the single most valuable source. After 1906, U.S. naturalization papers required the applicant's exact town of origin. These can unlock everything.

Passenger manifests after 1906 include a "last residence" column. Search the Ellis Island database or Ancestry for your ancestor's arrival record.

Death certificates, tombstones, and obituaries frequently contain birthplace information, sometimes down to the town level. Hebrew tombstone inscriptions in particular often name the deceased's place of origin.

Once you have a town name — even a badly misspelled one — you're ready to unlock JewishGen's most powerful tools.

ViewMate is JewishGen's free translation and transcription request service. You upload an image of the document, and volunteers from the community — many of them native speakers or trained researchers — will translate or transcribe it for you. It's one of JewishGen's most beloved features.

For help with Hebrew tombstone inscriptions specifically, JewishGen's InfoFiles include dedicated guidance on reading cemetery records. And if you'd like to learn the basics yourself, Online Classes cover document reading in several languages.
