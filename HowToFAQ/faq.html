The Global Scope

Not at all! While many of our users are based in the US or researching Eastern European Ashkenazi roots, our databases span the entire globe. We have dedicated research divisions for Sephardic and Mizrahi heritage, and millions of records covering South America, the UK, South Africa, Israel, North Africa, and the Middle East. If you have Jewish ancestry — no matter where they lived or where they settled — JewishGen is for you.

Family Lore vs. Hard Facts

Family stories are a starting point, not a finish line.

It might be! But in genealogy, family lore is treated as a clue, not a fact. Stories get exaggerated over generations. The only way to know for sure is to build your tree step-by-step, generation-by-generation, backing up every single link with historical documents.

Please don't! While online trees can provide excellent hints, they are notoriously full of errors. If one person makes a mistake, hundreds of others copy it. Use other people's trees as roadmaps, but always find the original records to prove the connections yourself.

Start with the paper trail they left behind in their new country. Before you look for records in Europe, exhaust the records created in their destination country. Look for their marriage licenses, death certificates, social security applications, and especially naturalization papers and passenger manifests.

The "Old Country"

The historical realities that often confuse modern researchers.

You haven't made a mistake — you've just discovered the Julian calendar! While most of the Western world used the Gregorian calendar, the Russian Empire used the older Julian calendar until 1918. In the 19th century, it ran 12 days behind the West; in the 20th century, 13 days behind.

The Pale of Settlement was a massive designated territory within the western borders of the Russian Empire. From the late 1700s until 1917, the vast majority of Jews in the Russian Empire were legally restricted to living only within this specific territory.

Welcome to the Polish Partitions! At the end of the 18th century, Poland was completely carved up by the Russian, Austro-Hungarian, and Prussian empires. It didn't exist as an independent country again until after World War I. If your ancestors lived in the Russian-controlled section, their official vital records were legally required to be written in Russian Cyrillic starting in 1868.

One of the biggest reasons our ancestors fled Eastern Europe.

It is highly likely! In 19th-century Imperial Russia, military conscription for Jewish men was brutally long and designed to force assimilation. To avoid this, many young men resorted to extreme self-mutilation — such as chopping off a "trigger finger" or starving themselves — so they would fail the mandatory medical exam.

Dates, Calendars & Cemeteries

Navigating the physical artifacts of Jewish history.

Jewish vital records, marriage contracts, and gravestones use the Hebrew calendar. A quick cheat code to roughly convert a Hebrew year in the 5000s to a Western year: drop the 5 and add 240. So 664 + 240 = 1904.

Jewish headstones follow a specific traditional formula. They typically start with פ״נ (Here lies). Next is the deceased's Hebrew given name, followed by "ben" (son of) or "bat" (daughter of), and then their father's Hebrew name. At the bottom you will usually see תנצבה (May their soul be bound up in the bond of life).

Uncovering Holocaust Records

Researching the Shoah is deeply emotional and historically complex.

Start directly with the JewishGen Holocaust Database. It is a massive collection drawn from concentration camp lists, ghetto registries, transport manifests, and post-war survivor lists. Because the Nazis kept meticulous — though ultimately incomplete — records, this is your best starting point for concrete documentation.

Do not give up. Hundreds of thousands of victims were murdered in mass shootings or extermination camps where records were destroyed or never kept. Your next essential stop is the Yad Vashem Central Database of Shoah Victims' Names, specifically searching for "Pages of Testimony" — brief memorial forms filled out by surviving friends and family members.

The Arolsen Archives is the world's most comprehensive physical archive on the victims and survivors of National Socialism, located in Germany. While JewishGen has partnered with them to index many records, their full digital archive is vast. If you find a reference to an ITS record on JewishGen, visit the Arolsen Archives website to see the original scanned documents.

JewishGen is primarily an archive of historical records. While our database includes post-war survivor registry lists, we do not actively track the current whereabouts of living individuals. To connect with descendants of survivors, your best tool is the JewishGen Family Finder (JGFF).
