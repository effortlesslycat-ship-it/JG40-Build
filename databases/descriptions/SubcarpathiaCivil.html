Vital Records · Subcarpathia · Ukraine · Hungary

Background

The JewishGen Subcarpathia Research Division focuses on parts of the former Hungarian megye (counties) of Bereg, Máramaros, Ugocsa and Ung. Following the First World War (via the 1920 Treaty of Trianon), these areas became the eastern-most part of the newly-formed nation of Czechoslovakia. Today, they are located in the Zakarpats’ka oblast of southwestern Ukraine.

The concept of Civil Registration—where everyone in a given town was registered by the government in the same book regardless of their religion—began in the Kingdom of Hungary in October 1895.

Source & Acquisition: With the help of a photographer in Ukraine, JewishGen is digitizing every Jewish record in these civil registration books. We estimate that there are 3,000 such books. These register books have not been microfilmed by the Mormons (LDS Church) yet, nor by the Ukrainian government. Other than actually traveling to the archives in Ukraine, or hiring a researcher to go there, the only way for genealogists and historians to research these records is through this JewishGen project.

Record Categories

The database compiles vital events recorded from 1895 to 1939. Currently, the collection encompasses the following major categories:

Births

Civil Birth Registers

Includes records of children born within the registered towns and surrounding villages. Early records typically contain more information regarding parents' birthplaces.

Over 218,000 records
Marriages

Civil Marriage Registers

Includes civil unions recognized by the state authorities across varying language periods (Hungarian, Slovak, and Ruthenian).

Over 30,000 records
Deaths

Civil Death Registers

Death certificates. Note that married women who died may be recorded under their married surname or their maiden surname interchangeably.

Over 91,000 records
As of August 2025, 2,092 books have been digitally photographed, and 2,079 books have been completely transcribed, making 417,248 records searchable via JewishGen.
Fields Recorded

While the specific details gathered by the government changed over time, the following fields are generally captured for individuals across the registers:

Surname & Given Name
Town of Event / Registration
Event Date (Birth, Marriage, or Death)
Father's Given Name & Surname
Mother's Given Name & Maiden Surname
Parents' Towns of Birth (Primarily pre-1906)
Spouse's Name (If applicable)
Book & Record/Page Number
Research Notes

This database reflects the shifting geopolitical borders and bureaucratic standards of the Subcarpathia region. The notes below explain language shifts, recording inconsistencies, and regional mapping.

Record Completeness (Pre-1906 vs. Post-1906)

Records from 1895 through the end of 1906 tend to contain much more detailed genealogical information than those recorded from 1907 onward, as the government began requesting less information.

The pre-1906 records are particularly valuable as they frequently include the towns of birth for the parents of a child being born or for individuals getting married.

Language Shifts in the Ledgers

Because the region changed hands multiple times, the language used by the registrars shifted according to the ruling authority:

  • 1895 – ~1919: Records are written in Hungarian.
  • ~1919 – 1939: Records switch to Slovak (during the Czechoslovakian period).
  • 1939 (Briefly): Records are written in Ruthenian (using the Cyrillic alphabet) for several months.
  • Post-1939: Records switch back to Hungarian following the border change.

Note: Some towns have random periods of Ruthenian-language entries interspersed during the Slovak era.

Missing Fathers & Unmarried Parents

When searching birth records, you may notice that many children do not have fathers listed. This was quite common, as it indicates the parents were not legally married under civil law at the time of the birth, even if they were married under religious law (chuppah).

Women's Surnames in Death Records

Important note: In the death records for this database, married women who died were recorded inconsistently. Sometimes they were recorded with their married surname, and sometimes with their maiden surname. Not every original record makes it explicitly clear whether the clerk was listing her maiden name or her married name.

Geographical Coverage

Registration books were maintained in a single, central town, but they generally covered vital events from that central town as well as several surrounding smaller villages. Sometimes the primary town responsible for a district was shifted by the government at a specific point in time.
