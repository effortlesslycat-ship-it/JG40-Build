Vital Records · Bucovina · Romania / Ukraine

Background

This database contains Jewish birth, marriage, divorce, and death records from Bucovina, a region whose archives reflect more than a century of shifting borders. Bucovina was a province of the Austrian Empire from 1775 until the end of World War I, which is why the records are written primarily in old German with dates given according to the Julian calendar.

Between the two world wars, Bucovina was part of Romania, divided into the counties (judeţe) of Cernăuţi, Câmpulung, Rădăuţi, Storojineţ, and Suceava. In 1940, as a result of the Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact, the northern half of Bucovina was annexed by the Soviet Union. Today, northern Bucovina lies in Ukraine (the western half of Chernivtsi Oblast), while southern Bucovina remains in northeastern Romania (most of Suceava County, plus a small sliver of Botoşani County).

The original registers are held at the Suceava County branch of the National Archives of Romania. Copies were obtained for the JewishGen Romania Research Division by a research assistant in Romania, and are made available for genealogical research only.

The Three Record Types

The database is organized around three register types kept by the civil registrars of Bucovina’s Jewish communities. Coverage by town and year is shown in the table further down this page.

Births

1843–1909

Detailed birth registers including parents, grandparents (where listed), mother’s maiden surname, place of birth, and witness names.

1834–1909

Research Notes

Two things to keep in mind when working with these records: the original handwriting and the calendar in use.

Reading the Records: Old German & the Julian Calendar

The original registers are written almost entirely in old German, often in Kurrentschrift or other 19th-century cursive hands. Some pages have Latin column headings with German entries beneath; multi-language headers (German, Latin, Romanian) appear in later registers. Handwriting quality varies widely, and some pages are extremely difficult to read.

Dates in these registers are recorded according to the Julian calendar, which through the 19th and early 20th centuries ran roughly 12–13 days behind the Gregorian calendar used today. When comparing a Bucovina date to a record from a Gregorian-calendar country, this offset must be accounted for.

Search Tips: Spelling Variations & Soundex

Names in these records were transliterated by clerks working in old German, often from Yiddish or Hebrew originals. Spelling varies considerably across registers and even within a single register.

We strongly recommend searching using the Daitch–Mokotoff Soundex as well as the exact name. Soundex matches names by sound rather than spelling, which is essential for German-language registers of Eastern European Jewish families.
